# Clinical Meaning of the 13 Dementia Model Labels

This note explains the 13 labels used in the dementia differential diagnosis paper from a clinical perspective.

The labels are best understood as **care-pathway buckets**. They describe what kind of impairment is present, what clinical process may be driving it, and what a clinician should consider checking next.

This is educational context only. It is not medical advice, diagnostic guidance, or a replacement for clinician judgment.

## Overview

The first three labels describe the patient's **cognitive state**:

- `NC`: Normal cognition
- `MCI`: Mild cognitive impairment
- `DE`: Dementia

The remaining ten labels describe possible **causes or contributors** to cognitive impairment:

- `AD`: Alzheimer's disease
- `LBD`: Lewy body dementia / Parkinson's disease dementia
- `VD`: Vascular dementia / vascular brain injury
- `PRD`: Prion disease
- `FTD`: Frontotemporal dementia / frontotemporal lobar degeneration
- `NPH`: Normal pressure hydrocephalus
- `SEF`: Systemic and environmental factors
- `PSY`: Psychiatric conditions
- `TBI`: Traumatic brain injury
- `ODE`: Other dementia etiologies

## 1. `NC` - Normal Cognition

Clinically means: no clear objective cognitive syndrome.

Typical pattern:

- Normal or near-normal memory, language, attention, executive function
- Independent in activities of daily living
- No meaningful decline from baseline

Clinical use:

- Reassurance, monitoring, prevention counseling
- Could still have subjective complaints, depression, sleep issues, medication effects, or early disease not captured yet

## 2. `MCI` - Mild Cognitive Impairment

Clinically means: objective cognitive decline, but not enough functional loss to call dementia.

Typical pattern:

- Memory, language, attention, visuospatial, or executive deficits
- Patient may still manage basic daily life
- Instrumental activities may be mildly affected, such as finances, medications, driving, complex work, or appointments

Key distinction:

- `MCI` = cognitive decline with mostly preserved independence
- `DE` = cognitive decline interfering with daily function

Why it matters:

- MCI can be stable, reversible, or progress to dementia
- It is often the stage where early intervention, monitoring, and biomarker workup matter most

## 3. `DE` - Dementia

Clinically means: cognitive impairment is severe enough to impair daily functioning.

Typical pattern:

- Decline in memory, reasoning, language, attention, judgment, visuospatial ability, or behavior
- Loss of independence in instrumental or basic activities
- Needs help with medications, finances, cooking, appointments, transportation, hygiene, or safety

Clinical use:

- Triggers care planning, safety review, caregiver support, and assessment of driving, medication, and financial safety
- Then clinicians ask: "What is causing the dementia?"

## 4. `AD` - Alzheimer's Disease

Clinically means: dementia or MCI pattern consistent with Alzheimer's pathology.

Typical presentation:

- Early episodic memory loss
- Repeating questions
- Misplacing items
- Trouble learning new information
- Word-finding difficulty later
- Disorientation, impaired judgment, functional decline

Common supporting clues:

- Amnestic cognitive profile
- Medial temporal or hippocampal atrophy on MRI
- Positive amyloid or tau biomarkers, if available
- APOE4 increases risk, but is not diagnostic

Why it matters:

- Alzheimer's-specific treatment eligibility may depend on stage and biomarkers
- Requires medication review, safety planning, and caregiver education
- In modern clinical workflows, biomarker confirmation is increasingly important for disease-modifying therapy decisions

## 5. `LBD` - Lewy Body Dementia / Parkinson's Disease Dementia

Clinically means: cognitive impairment related to Lewy body disease, including dementia with Lewy bodies and Parkinson's disease dementia.

Classic clues:

- Fluctuating cognition: good hours and bad hours
- Recurrent visual hallucinations
- REM sleep behavior disorder: acting out dreams
- Parkinsonism: bradykinesia, rigidity, tremor, shuffling gait
- Visuospatial and attention deficits may be prominent
- Memory may be less dominant early than in Alzheimer's disease

Clinical danger point:

- Patients can be sensitive to dopamine-blocking antipsychotics
- A high `LBD` signal should make clinicians ask carefully about hallucinations, sleep, falls, autonomic symptoms, and parkinsonism

## 6. `VD` - Vascular Dementia / Vascular Brain Injury

Clinically means: cognitive impairment caused or worsened by cerebrovascular disease.

Typical pattern:

- Executive dysfunction: planning, organization, processing speed
- Attention problems
- Stepwise decline after strokes, or gradual decline from small vessel disease
- Gait slowing, falls, mood changes, or personality changes can occur
- Memory may be less purely amnestic than in Alzheimer's disease

Supporting clues:

- Stroke or TIA history
- Hypertension, diabetes, atrial fibrillation, hyperlipidemia, smoking
- MRI findings such as white matter hyperintensities, lacunes, infarcts, or microbleeds

Why it matters:

- Vascular risk factors are actionable
- Management may include blood pressure, diabetes, lipids, smoking, anticoagulation, or antiplatelet decisions depending on cause

## 7. `PRD` - Prion Disease

Clinically means: concern for Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease or another prion disorder.

Clinical red flags:

- Rapidly progressive dementia over weeks to months
- Myoclonus: sudden jerks
- Ataxia or gait instability
- Visual symptoms
- Akinetic mutism in later stages
- Psychiatric or behavioral changes can occur

Supporting workup:

- MRI DWI/FLAIR abnormalities
- EEG
- CSF markers such as RT-QuIC, depending on setting
- Urgent neurology involvement

Why it matters:

- This is a medical urgency
- Very different tempo from typical Alzheimer's disease
- Infection-control or public health considerations may matter

## 8. `FTD` - Frontotemporal Dementia / Frontotemporal Lobar Degeneration

Clinically means: dementia syndrome involving frontal and/or temporal networks.

Major clinical forms:

- Behavioral variant FTD: disinhibition, apathy, loss of empathy, compulsions, poor judgment, dietary changes
- Primary progressive aphasia: progressive language impairment
- Motor-overlap syndromes: corticobasal syndrome, progressive supranuclear palsy, ALS overlap

Typical clues:

- Younger onset than Alzheimer's disease, often in the 50s or 60s
- Behavior or language changes before memory loss
- Executive dysfunction
- Frontal or anterior temporal atrophy

Why it matters:

- Often mistaken for psychiatric disease or Alzheimer's disease
- Family counseling and genetic considerations may matter
- Management is behavioral, safety-focused, and caregiver-focused

## 9. `NPH` - Normal Pressure Hydrocephalus

Clinically means: possible treatable dementia mimic caused by enlarged ventricles and cerebrospinal fluid dynamics.

Classic triad:

- Gait disturbance: magnetic, shuffling, difficulty initiating steps
- Urinary urgency or incontinence
- Cognitive impairment, often slowed thinking or executive dysfunction

Supporting clues:

- Ventriculomegaly on brain imaging
- Gait is often the earliest and most prominent symptom
- Tap test or lumbar drainage may help predict shunt response

Why it matters:

- Potentially treatable with shunting in selected patients
- High `NPH` should prompt imaging review and neurology or neurosurgery evaluation

## 10. `SEF` - Systemic and Environmental Factors

Clinically means: cognitive impairment may be driven by something outside a primary neurodegenerative dementia.

Examples:

- Delirium
- Infection
- Thyroid disease
- B12 deficiency
- Liver or kidney failure
- Sleep apnea
- Alcohol or substance use
- Medication toxicity, especially anticholinergics, sedatives, or opioids
- Metabolic abnormalities
- Autoimmune or inflammatory causes

Typical clues:

- Acute or fluctuating onset
- Systemic illness
- Polypharmacy
- Abnormal labs
- Reversible or partially reversible course

Why it matters:

- This is the "do not miss reversible causes" bucket
- A high `SEF` should trigger medication review, labs, delirium screen, substance history, sleep review, and medical evaluation

## 11. `PSY` - Psychiatric Conditions

Clinically means: cognitive symptoms may be primarily psychiatric, or psychiatric illness is significantly contributing.

Examples:

- Major depression
- Anxiety
- Bipolar disorder
- Schizophrenia
- PTSD
- Severe chronic stress
- Pseudodementia-like presentations

Typical clues:

- Prominent mood, anxiety, psychosis, or trauma symptoms
- Cognitive complaints may be inconsistent with objective testing
- Poor concentration and slowed processing
- Sleep, appetite, or energy changes
- Functional decline tied to psychiatric state

Important nuance:

- Psychiatric symptoms can mimic dementia
- Dementia can also cause psychiatric symptoms
- Both can coexist

Why it matters:

- Psychiatric illness may be a treatable contributor
- Needs careful distinction from FTD, LBD, delirium, substance effects, and medication effects

## 12. `TBI` - Traumatic Brain Injury

Clinically means: cognitive or behavioral impairment related to prior moderate/severe TBI, repetitive head injury, or chronic traumatic encephalopathy-like history.

Typical pattern:

- Attention and executive dysfunction
- Memory problems
- Irritability, impulsivity, depression, anxiety
- Sleep disturbance
- Headache, dizziness
- Personality or behavior changes

Supporting clues:

- History of significant head trauma
- Repetitive impacts from sports, military service, falls, or assaults
- Focal encephalomalacia or diffuse injury on imaging
- Symptoms linked temporally or cumulatively to injury history

Why it matters:

- Management includes neurorehabilitation, psychiatric support, sleep treatment, and safety planning
- It may coexist with Alzheimer's disease, vascular dementia, or psychiatric conditions

## 13. `ODE` - Other Dementia Etiologies

Clinically means: none of the above fits cleanly; consider less common neurological causes.

Examples from the paper:

- Brain tumors or neoplasms
- Down syndrome-associated dementia
- Huntington's disease
- Multiple system atrophy
- Seizure-related cognitive decline
- Other neurodegenerative or structural causes

Clinical clues:

- Atypical age of onset
- Focal neurologic signs
- Seizures
- Movement disorder
- Known genetic syndrome
- Structural lesion on imaging
- Course does not match AD, LBD, VD, or FTD patterns

Why it matters:

- Prevents forcing every patient into Alzheimer's or vascular categories
- High `ODE` should push clinicians toward broader neurologic workup

## How To Interpret The Labels Together

The first three labels answer:

```text
How impaired is the person?
NC vs MCI vs DE
```

The other ten answer:

```text
What clinical process might be causing or contributing to the impairment?
AD, LBD, VD, PRD, FTD, NPH, SEF, PSY, TBI, ODE
```

Example interpretations:

```text
High MCI + moderate AD
```

Possible early Alzheimer-pattern impairment, not yet dementia-level.

```text
High DE + high AD + moderate VD
```

Dementia syndrome, likely Alzheimer-pattern, with possible vascular contribution.

```text
High DE + high NPH
```

Dementia-level impairment with a potentially treatable hydrocephalus pattern, especially if gait and urinary symptoms are present.

```text
High DE + high PSY + high SEF
```

Cognitive impairment may be strongly influenced by psychiatric or systemic factors, so clinicians should avoid prematurely labeling it irreversible dementia.
